Plead guilty online

For some offences you may submit your guilty plea online, and not have to come to court.

  • You must be pleading guilty to ALL charges
  • Serious charges are not included
  • The case must be listed to be heard at the Neighbourhood Justice Centre

You should seek legal advice to help you decide

If you are charged with an offence you are considered innocent until found guilty. However, you have the option to plead guilty if you do not want to argue the charge.

If you have been charged with a minor offence and meet the criteria, you can plead guilty online. In most cases this means that you won't have to come to court.

Who can plead guilty online

Only a person who has received a summons and is required to appear at the Neighbourhood Justice Centre Magistrates Court in relation to a minor offence can plead guilty using this online form. If you decide to plead guilty online, in most cases you may not need to go to court.

You should seek legal advice to help you make your decision.

What you will need

When filling out this form, you will need the complaint and summons or notice to appear, which explains the details of the alleged offence and your court appearance details.

When to plead guilty online

You must submit your online guilty plea at least 2 business days before your court date.

If you submit an online guilty plea less than 2 business days before your court date, we will not be able to process it in time. In this case, you should contact NJC's registryto make sure we have received your plea of guilty and check if you still need to go to court.

What will happen in court

Your plea of guilty will be read out to the court. Depending on the circumstances of the offence and information given to the court by the prosecution, the magistrate may:

  • deal with the charge without you being there
    OR
  • require that you come to court and adjourn the matter to another date which you will be advised.

If you are required to go to court, there may be consequences if you fail to appear. For example, the magistrates may issue a warrant for your arrest.

f there is a possibility that your conviction will result in your driver licence being disqualified, your guilty plea will not be accepted and you will be notified of when you are required to attend court.

Who will advise you of the outcome

The court will let you know in writing of the new court date if the magistrate adjourns the matter. If the court emails you this advice, it will consider you have received it on the date the email is sent.

We will let you know in writing of any fine imposed by the magistrate. This may take up to 7 days after the court date.
